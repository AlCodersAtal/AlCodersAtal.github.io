Electronics Fundamentals

🟢 Beginner

⚡ Lesson 1: Voltage, Current & Resistance

Electricity sounds complicated, but it follows simple rules that you can understand with everyday analogies. Let's use water flow to explain it.

The Water Pipe Analogy

Electrical ConceptSymbolUnitWater Analogy
Voltage (V)VVolts (V)Water pressure — the "push" that drives electricity
Current (I)IAmperes (A)Water flow rate — how much electricity flows per second
Resistance (R)ROhms (Ω)Pipe narrowness — how much it opposes current flow
Power (P)PWatts (W)Energy used per second — V × I
💧
The Analogy Imagine water in a pipe. Voltage = the pump pressure pushing water. Current = how fast water flows. Resistance = how narrow or blocked the pipe is. High resistance → low current, just like a narrow pipe slows water flow.

In Arduino Terms

  • Your Arduino outputs 5V on its digital pins (or 3.3V on some boards)
  • Components like LEDs need current limited — we use resistors to do this
  • An LED typically needs about 20mA (milliamps = 0.02 Amperes)
  • Too much current = blown LED, or damaged Arduino pin
⚠️
Arduino Pin Limits Each Arduino Uno digital pin can source/sink a maximum of 40mA. The total current from ALL pins combined should not exceed 200mA. Always use resistors with LEDs!

🧮 Lesson 2: Ohm's Law

Ohm's Law is the most important formula in electronics. It connects voltage, current, and resistance. Master this and you can design any basic circuit.

Ohm's Law — Three Forms

Practical Example: LED Resistor Calculator

You want to connect an LED to Arduino's 5V pin. The LED has a forward voltage of 2V and needs 20mA current. What resistor do you need?

Formula 
Voltage across resistor = Supply voltage - LED forward voltage
                        = 5V - 2V = 3V

Required current = 20mA = 0.02A

Resistance = V / I
           = 3V / 0.02A
           = 150 Ohms

→ Use a 150Ω or 220Ω resistor (nearest standard value)
💡
Rule of Thumb For most standard 5mm LEDs with a 5V Arduino, a 220Ω resistor works perfectly. For 3.3V boards, use 100Ω. When in doubt, a higher resistance is safer than a lower one — your LED will just be slightly dimmer.

🧩 Lesson 3: Breadboards & Schematics

What is a Breadboard?

A breadboard is a plastic board with a grid of holes connected by metal strips underneath. You can push components and wires into these holes to build circuits without soldering. It's the prototyping tool every Arduino maker needs.

Breadboard Layout — Understanding the Connections

Breadboard Rules

  • 🔴 Red rail (+) — Connect to Arduino 5V or 3.3V
  • 🔵 Blue rail (−) — Connect to Arduino GND
  • All holes in the same row (e.g., row 5, a-e) are connected together
  • The center gap divides the board — no connection across it
  • Always match pin lengths to the right holes — short leg (−) to GND, long leg (+) to signal

Reading Schematics

A schematic is a diagram that shows how a circuit is connected using standard symbols. Learning to read schematics means you can follow any project in the world.

Memory Trick "Better Be Right Or Your Great Big Ventures Go Wrong" — Black, Brown, Red, Orange, Yellow, Green, Blue, Violet, Gray, White (0–9). A 220Ω resistor = Red-Red-Brown (2, 2, ×10 = 220Ω).

Capacitors

Capacitors store electrical energy temporarily and release it. They are used to smooth out power supply noise, filter signals, and in timing circuits.

TypePolarized?Common Use
Electrolytic✅ Yes (+ and − legs)Power filtering, large capacitance (µF range)
Ceramic❌ NoSignal decoupling, small capacitance (pF–nF)
Tantalum✅ YesStable, compact filtering
⚠️
Electrolytic Capacitors are Polarized! The long leg is (+) positive, short leg is (−) negative. The body also has a stripe marking the (−) side. Installing them backwards can cause them to explode. Always double-check orientation!

Golden Safety Rules

1
Never exceed 5V on digital/analog pins

Applying higher voltage directly to a pin will permanently damage the ATmega chip.

2
Always use resistors with LEDs

LEDs have almost no internal resistance — without a resistor, they draw unlimited current and burn out instantly.

3
Connect GND first, power last

When wiring, always connect the ground wire first. When disconnecting, remove power first.

4
Unplug before rewiring

Always disconnect USB before changing your circuit. A short circuit can destroy your Arduino instantly.

5
Check polarity

LEDs, electrolytic capacitors, and diodes have polarity. Installing them backwards can cause damage.

6
Avoid mains electricity (220V/110V)

Never connect your Arduino directly to wall power. Always use a properly rated adapter or relay module.
