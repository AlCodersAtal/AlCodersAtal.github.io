8.1 Capacitors in AC

TheoryBeginner

In AC circuits, capacitors continuously charge/discharge, allowing AC while blocking DC. This frequency-dependent resistance is capacitive reactance.

Xc = 1 / (2π × f × C)
High freq → low reactance (conducts). Low freq → high reactance (blocks). DC → infinite (fully blocks).

8.2 RC Time Constants

TheoryBeginner

An RC circuit charges/discharges exponentially. Time constant τ (tau) = R × C describes the speed.

τ = R × C (seconds)
After 1τ: 63% charged. After 5τ: 99% (fully charged)
💡 Example
R=10kΩ, C=100µF: τ = 1 second. Takes ~5 seconds to fully charge. Used in 555 timers, delays, and debouncing.

8.3 RC Filters

TheoryBeginner

Filters allow certain frequencies through while blocking others.

📌 Low-Pass Filter
R in series, C to ground. Passes low frequencies, blocks high. Cutoff: f_c = 1/(2πRC). Use to remove noise.
📌 High-Pass Filter
C in series, R to ground. Passes high frequencies, blocks DC. Use for AC coupling audio signals.

8.4 Inductors in Circuits

TheoryBeginner

Inductors store magnetic energy and oppose current changes. Their reactance increases with frequency.

XL = 2π × f × L
Inductive reactance — opposite of capacitive reactance
📌 Applications
DC-DC converters, EMI filters, transformers, LC oscillators for radio tuning.

8.5 Decoupling Capacitors

PracticalBeginner

Decoupling capacitors placed next to IC power pins stabilise supply voltage during switching transients — essential in every PCB design.

📌 Why They're Needed
ICs switch millions of times per second causing current spikes. A nearby capacitor acts as a local energy reservoir, preventing voltage drops.
💡 Standard Practice
100nF ceramic cap per IC between Vcc and GND, as close as physically possible to the power pin. Add 10µF electrolytic for power-hungry ICs.
