5.1 What is a Semiconductor?

TheoryBeginner

A semiconductor like silicon has conductivity between a conductor and insulator. Its conductivity is tuned by adding impurities (doping).

📌 N-type & P-type
N-type: doped with phosphorus, extra free electrons (negative carriers). P-type: doped with boron, creates holes (positive carriers).

5.2 The P-N Junction

TheoryBeginner

Joining P-type and N-type silicon creates a P-N junction with a depletion region. This is the basis of all diodes and transistors.

Built-in barrier ≈ 0.6–0.7V for Silicon
The potential current must overcome to flow forward
📌 Forward & Reverse Bias
Forward bias: reduces barrier, current flows. Reverse bias: increases barrier, current blocked.

5.3 Diode Types

TheoryBeginner

Different diodes serve different purposes. Choosing the right one matters.

🔩

Rectifier 1N4007

1A, 1000V, general purpose power rectification.

🔩

Zener Diode

Conducts in reverse at precise Zener voltage — voltage regulation.

🔩

Schottky 1N5819

Low forward voltage 0.3V, fast switching, power circuits.

🔩

Signal 1N4148

Fast, small, low current, signal processing.

5.4 Rectifier Circuits

PracticalBeginner

A rectifier converts AC to pulsating DC. Bridge rectifier (4 diodes) is the standard approach in power supplies.

📌 Bridge Rectifier
Four diodes in a diamond: both half-cycles of AC become positive DC. Add a large capacitor to smooth the pulsating output into steady DC.
💡 Output Voltage
Bridge rectifier output ≈ Vpeak − 1.4V (two diode drops). E.g. 9VAC → ~11.1V peak → ~9.7V DC after rectifier.

5.5 LEDs & Photodiodes

TheoryBeginner

LEDs emit light when forward biased. Photodiodes generate current when light hits them — the reverse process.

📌 LED Colours & Vf
Red: ~2V · Orange: ~2.1V · Yellow: ~2.2V · Green: ~2.1V · Blue/White: ~3.3V · UV: ~3.5V
💡 IR Pairs
IR LED + IR photodiode pairs form obstacle sensors and line sensors — used in the robot project in Module 12!
