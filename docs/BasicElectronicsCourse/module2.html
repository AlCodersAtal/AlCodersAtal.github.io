2.1 Resistors

TheoryBeginner

A resistor is the most common electronic component. Its job is to resist current flow, dropping voltage and limiting the current in a circuit. Resistors protect sensitive components from getting too much current.

2.2 Capacitors

TheoryBeginner

A capacitor stores electrical energy in an electric field, like a tiny rechargeable battery. Unlike batteries, capacitors charge and discharge almost instantly. They're used for filtering, timing, and smoothing power supplies.

C = Q / V
C = Capacitance (Farads, F) · Q = Charge stored (C) · V = Voltage across it

Types of Capacitors

🔵

Ceramic Capacitor

Small, cheap, non-polarised. Used for bypassing and decoupling. Values from pF to ~10µF.

🟤

Electrolytic Capacitor

Large capacitance (µF to mF). Polarised — has a positive and negative leg. Used in power supplies.

🟡

Film Capacitor

Precise, stable, non-polarised. Good for audio and timing circuits. More expensive.

⚠️ Polarity Warning
Electrolytic capacitors have a + and − side. Connect them backwards and they can explode! The negative leg is usually shorter and marked with a stripe.

Capacitance is measured in Farads (F), but one Farad is enormous. In practice you'll encounter:

  • µF (microfarad) = 10⁻⁶ F — common in power supplies
  • nF (nanofarad) = 10⁻⁹ F — common in signal circuits
  • pF (picofarad) = 10⁻¹² F — common in RF circuits

2.3 Inductors

TheoryBeginner

An inductor is simply a coil of wire. It stores energy in a magnetic field when current flows through it. It opposes changes in current — when current tries to increase, the inductor resists; when current tries to decrease, it tries to keep it flowing.

V = L × (dI/dt)
V = induced voltage · L = inductance (Henries, H) · dI/dt = rate of current change

Inductors are measured in Henries (H), but most practical inductors are in millihenries (mH) or microhenries (µH). They are used in:

  • Filters (in combination with capacitors — LC filters)
  • Transformers (two inductors sharing a magnetic core)
  • DC-DC converters (power supplies for circuits)
  • Radio circuits (tuning)
📌 For Beginners
You won't use inductors much in beginner projects. But understanding them is key for power supplies and filters. For now, just remember: capacitors resist voltage changes; inductors resist current changes.

2.4 Diodes & LEDs

TheoryBeginner

A diode is a one-way valve for electricity. Current can only flow through it in one direction — from anode (+) to cathode (−). If you try to push current the other way, the diode blocks it. This property is called rectification.

An LED (Light-Emitting Diode) is a special diode that produces light when current flows through it. LEDs are incredibly efficient and are used everywhere — from indicator lights to displays to room lighting. LEDs need a current-limiting resistor in series, or they will burn out instantly.

Calculating LED Resistor

R = (Vs − Vf) / If
Vs = supply voltage · Vf = LED forward voltage (~2V red, ~3.3V blue) · If = forward current (~20mA)
💡 Example
5V supply, red LED (Vf = 2V), 20mA current:
R = (5 − 2) / 0.02 = 3 / 0.02 = 150Ω. Use the nearest standard value: 150Ω or 220Ω.

2.5 Switches & Batteries

TheoryBeginner

Switches control whether a circuit is open (no current) or closed (current flows). Every button you press, every toggle you flip, is a switch. Types include:

  • SPST (Single Pole Single Throw) — simplest on/off switch
  • SPDT (Single Pole Double Throw) — selects between two paths
  • Push button (momentary) — only closed while pressed. Most common in projects.
  • Toggle switch — stays in position (latching)

Batteries

Batteries convert chemical energy to electrical energy. Key specifications:

  • Voltage (V): Determined by the chemistry. AA = 1.5V, Li-ion = 3.7V, 9V block = 9V.
  • Capacity (mAh): How much charge it holds. A 2000mAh battery can supply 2000mA for 1 hour, or 200mA for 10 hours.
  • Chemistry: Alkaline (disposable), NiMH (rechargeable), Li-ion (high density), LiPo (flexible shape).
Battery Life = Capacity (mAh) / Current Draw (mA)
Example: 1000mAh ÷ 50mA = 20 hours of operation
