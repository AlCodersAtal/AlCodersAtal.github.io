1.1 Atoms & Electrons

Theory Beginner

Everything around you — your phone, the lights in your room, even your body — relies on a single invisible particle: the electron. Understanding electronics starts here, at the atomic level.

The Atom

All matter is made of atoms. An atom has a central nucleus containing positively charged protons and neutral neutrons. Orbiting the nucleus are negatively charged electrons.

Simplified atom model — nucleus with orbiting electrons (e-)

Valence Electrons

The outermost electrons of an atom are called valence electrons. These are the electrons that can be knocked free and made to flow. Materials with loosely held valence electrons (like copper) are called conductors — electricity flows easily through them. Materials where electrons are tightly bound (like rubber) are called insulators.

🔵

Conductors

1–3 valence electrons. Copper, Silver, Gold, Aluminium. Electrons flow freely.

⚫

Insulators

5–8 valence electrons. Rubber, Plastic, Glass, Wood. Electrons don't flow.

🟣

Semiconductors

4 valence electrons. Silicon, Germanium. Can be made to conduct or insulate.

💡 Fun Fact
A single copper penny contains roughly 28,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000 free electrons — that's 28 septillion electrons available to carry current!

1.2 Electric Charge

TheoryBeginner

Electric charge is a fundamental property of matter. There are two types: positive (+) and negative (−). Like charges repel each other; opposite charges attract. This attraction and repulsion is the force that drives all of electronics.

Unit of Charge: The Coulomb

Charge is measured in Coulombs (C). One coulomb is the charge of approximately 6.24 × 10¹⁸ electrons. That's a huge number of electrons! In practice, we work with much smaller amounts of charge in everyday circuits.

Q = n × e
Q = total charge (C) · n = number of electrons · e = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C

Static vs. Current Electricity

Static electricity is charge that has built up and is not moving — like when you shuffle across carpet and then touch a doorknob. Current electricity is charge in motion — electrons continuously flowing through a conductor. Electronics deals almost entirely with current electricity.

📌 Remember
By convention, we say current flows from positive to negative (conventional current). But physically, electrons actually move from negative to positive. This historical convention can be confusing — just know both conventions exist!

1.3 Voltage & EMF

TheoryBeginner

Voltage is the electrical pressure that pushes electrons through a circuit. Think of it like water pressure in a pipe — the higher the pressure, the harder the water is pushed. Voltage is the "push" that makes electrons move.

The Water Analogy

💧

Water Pressure = Voltage

Higher voltage = more electrical pressure. Measured in Volts (V).

🌊

Water Flow = Current

How much charge flows per second. Measured in Amperes (A).

🪨

Pipe Friction = Resistance

Opposition to current flow. Measured in Ohms (Ω).

EMF — Electromotive Force

EMF (Electromotive Force) is the voltage produced by a source — like a battery or generator. A 9V battery provides 9 volts of EMF. It's the energy per unit charge that the source provides. EMF and voltage are often used interchangeably for beginners, but technically EMF is the source voltage and terminal voltage can differ due to internal resistance.

V = W / Q
V = Voltage (Volts) · W = Work done (Joules) · Q = Charge (Coulombs)
⚡ Common Voltages
AA Battery: 1.5V · Phone charger: 5V · Arduino: 5V · Car battery: 12V · Mains (India): 230V AC

1.4 Current & Amperes

TheoryBeginner

Electric current is the rate at which electric charge flows past a point in a circuit. When electrons move through a wire, that movement is current. The more electrons that pass per second, the higher the current.

I = Q / t
I = Current (Amperes, A) · Q = Charge (Coulombs) · t = Time (seconds)

DC vs. AC Current

Direct Current (DC): Electrons flow in one consistent direction. Batteries produce DC. Arduino runs on DC. Most electronic components use DC.

Alternating Current (AC): Electrons constantly reverse direction, typically 50 times per second (50 Hz in India). Mains power from the wall is AC. AC is used for long-distance power transmission because it's more efficient.

⚠️ Safety
Even small currents can be dangerous. Just 10 milliamperes (0.01A) through your heart can cause cardiac arrest. Never work with mains AC electricity without proper training. Always use batteries or low-voltage DC for your experiments.

1.5 Resistance & Conductors

TheoryBeginner

Resistance is the opposition a material offers to the flow of electric current. Even good conductors like copper have some resistance. The more resistance in a circuit, the harder it is for current to flow.

R = ρL / A
R = Resistance (Ω) · ρ = resistivity · L = length · A = cross-sectional area

Factors Affecting Resistance

  • Material: Copper has low resistivity; nichrome wire has high resistivity.
  • Length: Longer wire = more resistance (more obstacles for electrons).
  • Cross-section: Thicker wire = less resistance (more paths for electrons).
  • Temperature: Most conductors increase in resistance as they heat up.

The Three Quantities Together

Voltage (V), Current (I), and Resistance (R) are the three fundamental quantities in electronics. They are related by Ohm's Law, which we'll explore in depth in Module 3. For now, just remember:

  • Voltage pushes electrons (measured in Volts, V)
  • Current is the flow of electrons (measured in Amperes, A)
  • Resistance opposes the flow (measured in Ohms, Ω)
