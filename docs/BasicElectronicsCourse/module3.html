3.1 Ohm's Law

TheoryBeginner

Discovered by Georg Ohm in 1827, Ohm's Law is the single most important equation in all of electronics. It defines the relationship between voltage, current, and resistance.

V = I × R
Voltage (V) = Current (A) × Resistance (Ω)

This can be rearranged to solve for any of the three quantities:

  • To find Voltage: V = I × R
  • To find Current: I = V / R
  • To find Resistance: R = V / I
📌 What Ohm's Law means
If you double the voltage in a circuit but keep resistance the same, the current doubles. If you double the resistance but keep voltage the same, the current is halved. Voltage, current, and resistance are always mathematically linked.

Worked Examples

Example 1: Find Current
A 9V battery connected to a 470Ω resistor. What current flows?
I = V / R = 9 / 470 = 0.0191 A ≈ 19.1 mA
Example 2: Find Resistance
You want 10mA of current from a 5V supply. What resistor do you need?
R = V / I = 5 / 0.01 = 500Ω → use 470Ω (nearest standard value)
Example 3: Find Voltage
2mA flows through a 2.2kΩ resistor. What voltage drop is across it?
V = I × R = 0.002 × 2200 = 4.4V

3.3 Electric Power

TheoryBeginner

Power is the rate at which energy is consumed or produced in a circuit. A component consuming high power will get hot. This is why resistors have power ratings — exceed them and they burn out.

P = V × I
P = Power (Watts, W) · V = Voltage (V) · I = Current (A)

Using Ohm's Law, we can derive two more power formulas:

P = I² × R    P = V² / R
All three are equivalent — use whichever quantities you know

Power Ratings

Every component has a maximum power rating. Resistors commonly come in ¼W, ½W, 1W, 2W, 5W ratings. If a resistor dissipates more power than its rating, it will overheat and fail. Always design with some safety margin — use a resistor with at least 2× the calculated power.

3.4 Power Calculations

TheoryBeginner

Let's practice calculating power in real circuit situations.

Example: Resistor Power Rating
A 100Ω resistor with 12V across it:
P = V²/R = 144/100 = 1.44W
→ You need at least a 2W rated resistor (with safety margin).
Example: LED Power
An LED draws 20mA at 2V forward voltage:
P = V × I = 2 × 0.02 = 0.04W = 40mW
→ The LED consumes only 40 milliwatts. Very efficient!

Energy

Power × Time = Energy. Energy is measured in Joules (J) or Watt-hours (Wh).

E = P × t
E = Energy (J or Wh) · P = Power (W) · t = time (s or h)

3.5 Practical Examples

PracticalBeginner

Designing an LED Circuit

Let's use everything from this module to design a complete LED circuit from scratch.

Given: 5V supply (Arduino pin), red LED (Vf = 2V, If = 20mA)

  1. Voltage across resistor: Vr = 5 − 2 = 3V
  2. Required resistance: R = Vr / I = 3 / 0.02 = 150Ω
  3. Power in resistor: P = I² × R = (0.02)² × 150 = 0.06W = 60mW
  4. A standard ¼W (250mW) resistor is plenty — use a 150Ω ¼W resistor.
